Problem Statement  ·  IYA Format

An international student who feels overwhelmed about choosing a university abroad needs to compare programs, understand cultural expectations, and plan their transition but has no single trusted source of verified, personalized guidance.

STEP 01
The Problem

Something's broken — and everyone's used to it

Every year, millions of students leave home to study abroad. Most of them navigate that journey blind. University requirements are unclear. Programs are hard to compare across countries. Career outcomes are hidden.

And when students finally arrive? New grading systems, visa anxiety, loneliness, financial surprises. The support systems that exist: university advisors, orientation events, Facebook groups; are fragmented, impersonal, and reactive.

I made the biggest decision of my life based on a Reddit thread.

— Real interview quote, international student

The result: students enroll in the wrong programs, struggle to adapt, and waste time and money they can't get back. This wasn't an edge case. It was the norm.

STEP 02
The Opportunity

A market this big shouldn't have this gap

5M+intl. students in OECD
53%growth since 2014
$40Bannual US economic impact
0global student-led platforms

Demand for global education keeps growing. Students are more mobile than ever. And yet there's still no platform built specifically around the international student experience — one that covers pre-arrival through graduation, is student-driven, and provides verified information.

That's the white space we went after.

STEP 03
First Solution (Too Broad)

We tried to solve everything. First mistake.

Our initial concept was ambitious: a "Global Student Success Passport" — a digital ecosystem covering academics, wellness, logistics, social life, visa support, and safety, all in one place.

It was six products pretending to be one. Impossible to scope. Impossible to explain in a sentence.

This is a platform for everyone — which means it's really a platform for no one.

— Stakeholder review feedback, Week 3

Back to the drawing board. And into primary research.

STEP 05
Final Solution · UVP + VVP

One clear sentence changed everything.

The research gave us permission to cut. We stopped trying to be everything and committed to the highest-signal insight: students don't just need information, they need a trusted person who's been in their exact situation.

Mento

We help universities support international students through a verified mentorship app for personalized, trusted guidance.

Core features (prioritized from research):

AI-powered mentor matching : peer + alum + faculty, matched by major, background, goals
Visa & immigration hub : step-by-step OPT/CPT guidance with milestone reminders
Community hubs : topic-based spaces: housing, internships, wellness
24/7 resource library short video explainers and checklists
University analytics dashboard : engagement and retention tracking for institutions
STEP 06
Customer Profile

We sell to universities. Students are the beneficiaries.

Mento is B2B. The paying customer is the institution, not the student. This distinction shaped every pricing and GTM decision we made.

University International Offices
  • Overextended teams trying to improve onboarding + retention
  • Mento gives them scalable mentorship and actionable data
  • Helps them extend support pre-arrival, not just post-orientation
Education Consultants & Exchange Programs
  • Their support typically ends at admission
  • Mento extends their value into the student's actual transition
  • Verified mentorship + digital tools across institutions
Corporate Sponsors & Partner Organizations
  • Support cultural exchange and student mobility programs
  • Mento offers purpose-driven partnerships tied to student wellbeing
STEP 07
Market Analysis · TAM / SAM / SOM

The market is large — and underpenetrated.

We focused our go-to-market on the 19% of institutions with the highest international student enrollment — the ones already spending on retention, with clear budget lines for international student services.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

01
Involve engineering earlier
We nearly built a matching algorithm that was out of scope for our timeline. A feasibility check in week 2 would have saved weeks of rework — and taught me to always scope technically before committing to a feature.
02
Stakeholder feedback is ongoing, not a final review
Our biggest pivots came from reviews we should have scheduled earlier. Getting stakeholders in the room at week 2 — not week 6 — would have sharpened our direction much faster.
03
Narrow sooner
The research gave us clear signal early, but we held onto the broader concept longer than we needed to. Cutting sooner isn't failure — it's focus. The final product was stronger for it.
